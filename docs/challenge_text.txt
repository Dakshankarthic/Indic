AutoAnn-Indic
AutoAnn-Indic: Human-Effort Efficient Automated Annotation for Indic Manuscripts
and Ram Charit Manas
Challenge Proposal for NCVPRIPG 2026
=== PAGE 2 ===
1. Introduction
Indic manuscripts—on paper as well as palm-leaf—exhibit extreme variability in script and calligraphic 
styles, ink density, background texture, page geometry, and complex degradations such as bleed-through, 
stains, cracks, holes, fading, marginal notes, ornaments, and inconsistent ruling. Although large-scale 
digitization has produced extensive image collections, progress in Indic Document AI remains bottlenecked 
by the lack of high-quality annotations for core structures such as layout regions, text lines/baselines, 
damage masks, and reading order. Creating such ground truth manually is slow, costly, and expertisedependent, which prevents scaling datasets to the sizes needed for robust OCR/HTR, retrieval, and 
preservation pipelines.
AutoAnn-Indic proposes a challenge on automated annotation generation for Indian cultural heritage 
documents, combining diverse Indic manuscripts with a focused, culturally significant subset—Ramcharit 
Manas—to represent a realistic deployment scenario. The objective is to develop methods that produce 
usable pre-annotations directly from raw page images with minimal supervision, enabling humans to 
finalize annotations rapidly. Participants will be provided (i) a curated collection of manuscript pages across 
styles and degradation levels (including Ramcharit Manas pages), (ii) a small seed set of expert-annotated 
pages, and (iii) a larger unlabeled pool to encourage weak supervision, self-training, and active learning. 
Teams will submit predictions in a standard format (e.g., PAGE-XML or COCO polygons) for tasks including 
layout parsing (text regions, marginalia/notes, illustrations/diagrams, page/frame, and damage/holes) and 
text-line/baseline extraction.
The key novelty of AutoAnn-Indic is its practical, human-centered evaluation. Beyond measuring 
annotation quality, we explicitly evaluate human effort saved. On a hidden evaluation subset, submitted 
pre-annotations will be corrected by trained annotators using a fixed tool and protocol. On a hidden 
evaluation subset, submitted pre-annotations will be corrected by trained annotators using a fixed tool and 
protocol. We will record correction time per page (seconds/page). Rankings will therefore reflect both 
accuracy and annotation efficiency, rewarding solutions that are not only correct but also fast to finalize.
By benchmarking quality–cost trade-offs in a realistic setting, AutoAnn-Indic aims to catalyze scalable 
ground-truth generation pipelines, robust learning under severe manuscript degradations, and practical 
human-in-the-loop document understanding—accelerating the creation of high-value annotated datasets 
for Indic cultural heritage.
2. Impact & significance 
AutoAnn-Indic addresses the primary bottleneck in Indic manuscript AI: scalable creation of reliable 
ground-truth annotations. While advances in layout analysis and HTR/OCR have improved model 
performance, real adoption on manuscripts remains limited due to the high cost, slow pace, and expertise 
required for annotation—especially under severe degradations and high stylistic diversity. By reframing the 
problem as automated annotation generation (rather than only recognition), and by including both a 
diverse Indic manuscript corpus and a focused, culturally significant subset (Ramcharit Manas), this 
challenge directly targets real-world dataset building and deployment needs.
Research Impact
=== PAGE 3 ===
• A practical benchmark beyond accuracy: Most document challenges evaluate only segmentation 
or OCR accuracy. AutoAnn-Indic introduces a Quality + Human-Effort evaluation, encouraging 
methods that generate usable pre-annotations that are fast to finalize—an essential step for 
building large datasets.
• Advances in low-label learning: The setup of small seed labels + large unlabeled pool naturally 
promotes research in weak supervision, self-training, semi-supervised learning, uncertainty-aware 
predictions, and active learning—methods needed for heritage collections where labels are scarce.
• Robustness under real degradations: Manuscripts frequently exhibit holes/tears, bleed-through, 
ink spread, fading, warped pages, and non-uniform layouts. The benchmark explicitly drives 
solutions that generalize across materials (paper/palm-leaf), writing styles, and damage patterns—
where standard document models often fail.
• Stronger downstream pipelines: High-quality auto-annotations unlock better training data for 
OCR/HTR, reading-order modeling, keyword spotting, retrieval, and translation workflows—
improving end-to-end manuscript understanding and restoration pipelines.
Industry and Societal Impact
• Direct value for libraries and archives: Methods that reduce correction time can be integrated into 
digitization workflows, enabling institutions to convert large collections into structured, searchable 
corpora faster and at lower cost.
• Cultural heritage preservation and access: Improved annotations accelerate transcription and 
indexing, supporting scholars, educators, and the public in discovering and preserving Indic 
knowledge systems. The inclusion of Ramcharit Manas strengthens cultural relevance and 
showcases a high-value, real deployment case.
• Reusable community assets: The challenge will release standardized formats (e.g., PAGEXML/COCO), evaluation scripts, and annotation guidelines, creating a long-lived benchmark and 
tooling ecosystem that can be adopted beyond the competition.
Overall, AutoAnn-Indic positions NCVPRIPG’26 as a platform for a high-impact, practice-oriented 
Document AI challenge that aligns research novelty with realistic digitization and annotation requirements 
for Indian cultural heritage.
3. Major rules for participation & evaluation criteria
A) Major Rules for Participation
1. Data Policy (Core):
Participants may train using only the officially released AutoAnn-Indic dataset, consisting of a 
seed-labeled set and a larger unlabeled pool (covering both Indic manuscripts and Ramcharit 
Manas pages).
2. External Pretraining (Allowed with Full Disclosure):
Use of publicly available pretrained models/weights is allowed only if fully disclosed, including: 
model name, source link, and any external data used during pretraining. Use of private, proprietary, 
or undisclosed datasets is not permitted.
=== PAGE 4 ===
3. No Additional Manual Labeling:
Teams must not manually annotate any additional pages beyond the released seed labels. Any 
human labeling effort outside the provided seed set is prohibited to ensure fairness.
4. Submission Outputs & Format:
Submissions must follow the official schema and page identifiers, using PAGE-XML and/or COCOstyle JSON (final format confirmed at data release). Required outputs include:
o Layout regions (instance polygons/masks) with class labels (e.g., text_region, 
marginalia/notes, illustration/diagram, page_frame, damage/hole).
o Text lines as line polygons and optional baselines as polylines.
5. Submission Limits:
To prevent test-set overfitting, the number of test submissions will be limited (e.g., 3–5 
submissions per team). Validation submissions will be allowed as per organizer policy.
6. Reproducibility (Required for Winner Verification):
Shortlisted teams must provide a brief method description and runnable inference instructions 
(script/container) to enable verification.
7. Fair Use & Ethics:
The dataset is provided strictly for research and challenge participation. Teams must respect 
dataset licensing, avoid attempts to re-identify sources beyond provided metadata, and ensure 
submissions/releases do not contain offensive, harmful, or sensitive content.
B) Evaluation Criteria
AutoAnn-Indic evaluates submissions on two complementary dimensions: annotation quality and 
human effort required to finalize annotations.
1) Annotation Quality Score (Q)
Computed on the hidden test set using official evaluation scripts.
Track 1: Layout Auto-Annotation
• Region segmentation quality: mIoU and/or mask mAP (as applicable)
• Instance-level quality: Panoptic Quality (PQ) where applicable
Track 2: Text-Line / Baseline Auto-Annotation
• Line extraction: Line IoU and/or Line F1-score
• Baseline accuracy: tolerance-based baseline distance score (if baselines are provided)
Note: The final class list, output schema, and official metric definitions will be released with the 
dataset and evaluation toolkit.
2) Human Effort Score (E) — Time-only
Measured on a hidden Human-in-the-Loop subset. Trained annotators will correct each team’s preannotations to match the official annotation guidelines using a fixed tool and protocol (same UI, 
same instructions). We record:
𝐸 = Median(Time/page)
=== PAGE 5 ===
where Time/page is measured in seconds per page. Median is used to reduce the impact of outliers 
(extremely hard pages).
Fairness controls: same annotator pool across teams, randomized page order, and a balanced mix 
of both domains (Indic manuscripts + Ramcharit Manas) and degradation levels.
3) Final Ranking
We will provide one of the following transparent approaches:
Pareto Ranking
Leaderboard highlights methods that dominate others in both higher quality (Q) and lower effort 
(E).
Combined Score (Single Number)
𝑆 = 𝑄 × exp (−𝜆𝐸)
(or an equivalent normalized weighted combination; 𝜆 published in advance).
Tie-breakers:
1. Higher performance on the Hard Subset (severe bleed-through, holes/tears, low contrast),
2. Lower human effort (E),
3. Faster inference runtime (optional, if standardized).
4. Novelty of the Challenge Proposal
AutoAnn-Indic is novel and differentiated from existing document AI challenges in the following key ways:
1. Auto-annotation as the central objective:
Instead of framing the task as accuracy-only layout analysis or OCR/HTR, AutoAnn-Indic explicitly 
targets automated annotation generation—producing high-quality pre-annotations that can be 
converted into final ground truth efficiently. This directly addresses the largest practical barrier in 
Indic manuscript AI: scalable label creation.
2. Human-in-the-loop, quality–cost evaluation:
The challenge introduces a human-centered leaderboard that jointly measures (i) annotation 
quality and (ii) human effort required to finalize labels, quantified via median correction time per 
page (seconds/page) under a fixed tool and protocol. This “quality–cost” benchmark prioritizes 
outputs that are not only accurate but also fast to finalize, making them directly usable in real 
digitization and annotation workflows.
3. Realistic low-label learning with domain diversity (Indic + Ramcharit Manas):
AutoAnn-Indic provides a small seed-labeled set and a larger unlabeled pool, encouraging weak 
supervision, self-training, semi-supervised learning, and uncertainty-aware predictions—methods 
necessary for heritage collections where labels are scarce. Including both diverse Indic manuscripts
=== PAGE 6 ===
and a focused, culturally significant subset (Ramcharit Manas) strengthens novelty by enabling 
evaluation across domain variation and showcasing a high-impact, real deployment scenario.
Together, these elements make AutoAnn-Indic a practice-oriented and research-relevant challenge that 
advances scalable dataset creation for Indic cultural heritage rather than merely optimizing recognition 
accuracy.
